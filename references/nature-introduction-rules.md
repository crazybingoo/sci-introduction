# Nature-Style Introduction Rules

Purpose: provide generic, project-neutral rules for planning, diagnosing, drafting, and rewriting SCI manuscript Introductions in a Nature-style funnel.

## Required Starting Point

Drafting or rewriting an Introduction requires the manuscript body or a detailed body-level outline. The body should make clear what was done, what was found, what evidence supports the claims, and what the work does not claim.

Do not draft from a title, abstract, topic phrase, or method name alone. Ask for the manuscript body or a body outline first.

## Video-Guided Logic Gate

Before drafting, answer three questions:

1. Why is this field, problem, system, or task important?
2. What specific problem remains unresolved?
3. What does this study investigate, test, build, or explain?

Then check those answers against the manuscript body:

- The importance sentence should be broad but short.
- The unresolved problem should be narrow enough that the study can answer it.
- The present-study move should match the actual methods and evidence.
- The final claim should be bounded by the strongest supported findings.

## Standard Funnel

1. **Field stake.** One crisp opening that gives the reader a reason to care.
2. **Bottleneck.** The core difficulty that makes the problem unsolved.
3. **Prior attempts and residual gap.** A synthesis of what existing work can and cannot do.
4. **Present study.** The study's direct response to the gap.
5. **Evidence and contribution.** A grouped preview of evidence and a restrained final claim.

Use 4-6 paragraphs as needed. Five paragraphs is the default, not a rule.

## Drafting Constraints

- Keep broad background short.
- Prefer synthesis over author-by-author literature listing.
- Use transitions that show narrowing: field -> subfield -> unresolved problem -> present study.
- Include method detail only when needed to understand the gap or present-study move.
- Preview evidence classes rather than figures, tables, or numerical results.
- Avoid vague endings such as "provides new insights" unless the sentence states what kind of insight.
- Avoid overclaiming beyond the manuscript body.

## Diagnosis Checklist

- Does the first paragraph reach the real problem quickly?
- Is the unresolved problem specific and answerable by this study?
- Does prior work appear as synthesis rather than a list?
- Does the "Here we" sentence directly solve the named gap?
- Are methods introduced as a response to the gap rather than as an inventory?
- Does the final paragraph preview evidence by class?
- Are claims, terminology, and scope consistent with the manuscript body?
