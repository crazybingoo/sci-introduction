## Core Message

The video's central advice is to avoid an overlong generic setup in an Introduction. A practical Introduction should quickly answer three questions:

1. Why is this field or topic important?
2. What problem remains unresolved?
3. What does this study investigate?

Use this as a compact writing heuristic, not as a rigid journal rule.

## Suggested Paragraph Logic

1. **Open with one broad-background sentence.**
   - State the large field or problem directly.
   - Example pattern: `X has become a global / major / clinically important concern.`
   - Do not spend several paragraphs proving that the field matters.

2. **Narrow into the subfield and current approaches.**
   - Move from the broad issue to the smaller research area.
   - Name what current methods or approaches do for that area.
   - The video example uses an "among which" move to introduce the main method or protagonist approach.

3. **Summarize progress and expose the research gap.**
   - Briefly describe what previous studies have done.
   - The video illustrates this with a compact sequence of prior-study moves, then turns to the gap.
   - A useful transition is: `Despite these advances, several challenges remain.`
   - List only the gap points that the current study will actually answer.

4. **State what the present study does.**
   - End the Introduction setup with a direct study-aim paragraph.
   - Use a structure like: `Therefore, this study aims to investigate X. Specifically, we...`
   - Close with the kind of insight the study is expected to provide, without overclaiming.

## How To Apply With Nature Style

- Keep the video's short-setup principle.
- Replace formulaic author-by-author listing with Nature-style synthesis whenever possible.
- Keep the broad background to one crisp sentence unless the audience truly needs more context.
- Turn "previous studies did A/B/C" into a bottleneck statement: what remains hard to know, measure, infer, explain, or validate?
- Use the final paragraph to preview evidence classes rather than writing a full result roadmap.
- Avoid vague value claims such as "provide new insights" unless the sentence says what kind of insight.

## Skill Usage Rules

- Treat the three questions as a required pre-drafting logic gate.
- Apply the questions to the manuscript body, not to the abstract alone.
- If the manuscript body is missing, ask for it before drafting.
- Use the answers to build a one-line argument before writing prose.
- Keep the Introduction focused on the unresolved problem that the current study actually answers.
